Bats

When you hear the word bat, what do you think of? Count dracula? A vampire? Transylvania? Echolocation? Well, they are also excellent pollinators. Many of these pollinator bats are usually found in other parts of the world. There are over 300 species or fruit that are actually dependent on these bats. Some are in Mexico and the southern United states, however the vast majority live in Africa, southeast Asia, and the Pacific Islands. Since bats are nocturnal, they only come out in the night. This is helpful to flowers that bloom during the night, because during the day they might not be pollinated by other pollinators since they do not open up at that time.

Although the pollinating bats are very important to hundreds of species of plants, other types of bats are useful too. Many other species of bats eat insects, this can prove useful to many farms because they will eat the most destructive of pests. Pregnant mothers can eat up to their whole body weight in insects! These bats are also important for the quality of the soil, their droppings can help to fertilize the soil which can make it richer and healthier for the crops. Most bats will usually go for quite large flowers that are very pale, or smell fermented like fruits. The fruits they mainly pollinate include, mangoes, bananas, and mangoes.

The Mexican Long-Tongued Bat

The Mexican long-tongued bat is one of the few pollinating bat species in North America. It is typically found in Mexico, hence that part of their name, but is also found in much of the southwestern United States of America, specifically in Texas, New Mexico, Arizona and California. These bats are usually three inches tall, and their tongues are about a third of their body, making them typically one inch. They were previously endangered in 1988 however made a speedy recovery, this was due to lots of Agave harvesting, which is used to make liquor, and the fact that Agave plants do not lastthat long.

Lesser Long-Nosed Bat

The Lesser long-nosed bat is another one of the few pollinating bat species in North America. It is also predominantly found in Mexico. However they can also thrive in the southern United States, typically in Arizona and New Mexico. These bats are usually 3 inches in size, with a wingspan of 10 inches. What helps then with the pollination is their long tongue, it is the size of their body! This helps them reach the nectar in the flowers. These bats were also previously endangered but taken off the list in 2018.
